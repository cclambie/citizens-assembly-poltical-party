# Democratic Campaign Equalisation, Participation and Civic Access Framework
## Draft for discussion

### Preamble

Democracy functions best when political participation is not determined primarily by wealth, but by the collective engagement of citizens and communities.

This framework seeks to:

- Reduce the disproportionate influence of concentrated wealth.
- Encourage genuine grassroots participation.
- Equalise campaign opportunities between electorates with differing economic capacities.
- Recognise time, labour, expertise, and community effort as democratic contributions alongside financial donations.
- Ensure political access remains subject to public scrutiny.
- Prevent actual and perceived "pay for access" corruption.
- Strengthen transparency, accountability, and public trust.

The framework is based upon a simple principle:

> Political opportunity should be determined by democratic participation, not by wealth alone.

## Principle 1: Democratic Campaign Equalisation Trust (DCET)

A permanent independent Democratic Campaign Equalisation Trust shall be established.

The Trust shall:

- Receive and administer major donations.
- Operate campaign equalisation mechanisms.
- Administer participation credits.
- Publish transparency reports.
- Conduct audits.
- Maintain public confidence in the system.

The Trust shall operate independently from political parties, candidates, governments, and donors.

### Trustees

Trustees must not:

- Hold elected office.
- Be candidates.
- Hold party executive positions.
- Receive campaign remuneration.

Trustees shall be appointed through an independent process designed to maximise public confidence.

## Principle 2: Separation of Major Donors from Candidates

Large corporations, multinational corporations, major philanthropic foundations, and high-net-worth donors shall not donate directly to candidates.

Such donations must be made exclusively to the Democratic Campaign Equalisation Trust.

Major donors may not:

- Direct funds to specific candidates.
- Direct funds to specific electorates.
- Direct funds to factions or internal groups.
- Attach policy conditions.
- Request preferential treatment.

All donations shall be deemed unconditional support for democratic participation.

## Principle 3: Campaign Capacity Index (CCI)

Campaign equalisation shall be based upon a Campaign Capacity Index rather than solely on measures of disadvantage.

The purpose of the Index is to equalise campaign opportunity, not merely redistribute resources.

### Factors Considered

#### Economic Capacity

Using ABS and other public data:

- Median household income.
- Disposable household income.
- Cost of living.
- Housing affordability.
- Business activity levels.
- Employment rates.

#### Social Capacity

- SEIFA indicators.
- Educational attainment.
- Volunteer participation rates.
- Community organisation density.
- Civic participation measures.

#### Electoral Capacity

- Historical election margins.
- Opposition spending patterns.
- Number of active candidates.
- Geographic size.
- Population mobility.
- Regional and rural challenges.

The Index shall be reviewed before each election cycle and published in full.

## Principle 4: Community Matching Model

Candidates and endorsed local campaigns access Equalisation Trust funding only through demonstrated community support.

### Eligible Matching Contributions

#### Individuals

Up to $500 per individual annually.

#### Local Businesses

Businesses employing fewer than 20 employees.

Up to $2,000 annually.

#### Community Organisations

Locally operating not-for-profit organisations.

Up to $5,000 annually.

### Matching Rates

Matching rates shall be determined by the Campaign Capacity Index.

Examples:

- High-capacity electorates: 1:1
- Medium-capacity electorates: 2:1
- Lower-capacity electorates: 3:1

A $100 local contribution may therefore generate between $100 and $300 in additional support.

## Principle 5: Donor Categories

### Community Donors

- Individuals.
- Local businesses.
- Local not-for-profit organisations.

Eligible for matching.

### Regional Donors

- Medium-sized businesses.
- Regional philanthropic organisations.

May contribute directly but may receive reduced matching treatment.

### Institutional Donors

- Corporations with more than 1,000 employees.
- Multinational corporations.
- National corporations.
- Major philanthropic foundations.

May contribute only to the Democratic Campaign Equalisation Trust.

They may not donate directly to candidates.

## Principle 6: Democratic Participation Credits

Time contributed to democratic participation shall be recognised as a contribution equivalent to financial support.

### Eligible Activities

Activities may include:

- Door knocking.
- Phone banking.
- Community outreach.
- Event organisation.
- Administrative support.
- Policy consultation.
- Local issue campaigns.
- Digital campaigning.
- Community engagement activities.
- Other approved campaign activities.

Activities must be:

- Verifiable.
- Recorded.
- Auditable.

### Valuation

Volunteer contributions shall generate Democratic Participation Credits at a rate equal to twice the applicable minimum wage.

Example:

If minimum wage is $25 per hour:

- One volunteer hour = $50 Participation Credit.

Ten volunteer hours:

- $500 Participation Credits.

### Purpose

Participation Credits may be used to unlock Equalisation Trust matching support.

No cash payment shall be made to volunteers.

Credits exist solely for democratic equalisation purposes.

## Principle 7: Community Campaign Recognition

Participation Credits may only be earned through recognised democratic activities.

Recognition may be granted through:

- Candidate endorsement.
- Local representative endorsement.
- Community campaign committees.
- Citizens' panels.
- Deliberative assemblies.
- Local membership ballots.
- Other approved democratic processes.

The purpose is to ensure that community effort supports genuine democratic activity.

## Principle 8: Volunteer Integrity Safeguards

To maintain public confidence:

### Verification

All volunteer hours must be:

- Logged.
- Verified.
- Available for audit.

### Maximum Recognised Hours

The Trust may establish maximum weekly recognition limits unless special approval is granted.

### Independent Certification

Individuals may not certify their own hours.

Immediate family members may not certify each other's hours.

### Audits

Random audits shall be conducted regularly.

Fraudulent claims shall result in disqualification from the system.

## Principle 9: Transparency

All donations shall be publicly reported.

### Donations Above $200

Published information:

- Donor name.
- Donation amount.
- Date.
- Recipient category.

### Donations of $200 or Less

Reported in aggregate form.

Example:

"158 community donations totalling $10,420."

This protects ordinary citizens while preserving transparency.

## Principle 10: Civic Access Equalisation

Political access is recognised as a democratic resource.

Where donors receive access to candidates, elected representatives, ministers, shadow ministers, or senior party officials, equivalent community access must also be provided.

No event may provide exclusive political access to donors.

## Principle 11: Community Oversight at Access Events

Where corporations, foundations, industry groups, or major donors purchase attendance at fundraising events, dinners, forums, briefings, meetings, or similar engagements:

At least 50 percent of all available places at the relevant table, discussion group, or engagement setting must be allocated to community representatives.

Community representatives may be selected through:

- Civic lottery.
- Citizens' juries.
- Community nomination.
- Open public application.
- Deliberative selection processes.

Community representatives shall receive:

- Equal seating.
- Equal participation rights.
- Equal speaking opportunities.
- Equal access to information and discussion.

## Principle 12: No Private Influence Rule

No donor-funded event may facilitate private lobbying or exclusive conversations with elected representatives or candidates.

At all times:

- Community representatives must be present.
- Conversations must remain observable.
- Significant policy discussions must be capable of public reporting.

Political access shall remain subject to civic scrutiny.

## Principle 13: Civic Primacy Principle

Attendance by corporate, philanthropic, or institutional donors does not confer status, authority, influence, ownership, or special rights.

The role of institutional donors is to support democratic participation, not purchase influence.

Community representatives are present as equal participants and democratic scrutineers.

The governing principle is:

> Donors do not purchase access to representatives.
>
> Donors support democratic forums in which citizens and representatives can engage together.

No participant may claim greater entitlement due to financial contribution.

## Principle 14: Matching Access Requirement

Where a donor purchases attendance, sponsorship, or participation rights:

Equivalent civic participation must be funded simultaneously.

The donor's contribution shall be deemed to support:

- Their own participation.
- Community participation.
- Democratic oversight.

The presence of community representatives is not optional.

It is a mandatory condition of access.

## Principle 15: Redistribution of Unused Capacity

Where a campaign does not utilise its full equalisation entitlement:

Unused capacity shall return to the Trust.

The Trust may redistribute surplus resources to:

- High-performing grassroots campaigns.
- Emerging candidates.
- Under-resourced electorates.
- Strategic electoral opportunities.

All redistribution decisions shall be publicly reported.

## Principle 16: Annual Reporting

The Trust shall publish annual public reports detailing:

- Total donations received.
- Donor categories.
- Matching allocations.
- Campaign Capacity Index calculations.
- Participation Credits awarded.
- Volunteer participation levels.
- Access event participation.
- Audit outcomes.
- Redistribution decisions.

Reports shall be publicly accessible.

## Principle 17: Public Review and Deliberation

The framework shall be reviewed periodically through public deliberative processes.

Reviews may include:

- Citizens' juries.
- Community assemblies.
- Independent expert panels.
- Public consultation.

The objective is continuous democratic improvement.

## Foundational Democratic Test

Every decision, allocation, event, and activity undertaken under this framework must satisfy the following test:

> Does this increase democratic participation, equalise political opportunity, strengthen civic oversight, and reduce the ability of wealth alone to determine political influence?

If the answer is no, the activity shall not be supported by the Democratic Campaign Equalisation Trust.

## Public Summary

A simple explanation for voters:

- Large donors cannot choose candidates.
- Communities unlock campaign funding.
- Volunteer effort counts alongside money.
- Funding is matched according to local circumstances.
- Political access is shared with citizens.
- No private lobbying through fundraising.
- Significant donations are transparent.
- Democracy works best when money is balanced by participation.
